A Collection of Colors with Identity

Artista understands it can be frustrating to name and describe colors. Often, the same color description can bear a different meaning to different people and reference points are necessary to eliminate ambiguities.
With the goal to create a high-quality point of reference, Artista has studied and collected data from multiple sources to narrow it down to a concise dictionary of 1,800 distinctly unique colors associated with a maximum overall color space coverage.


A Drop in the Ocean?

Although 1,800 may appear like a big number, it comprises only 1% of all colors that can be defined in the 24-bit RGB (red, green, blue) web space - namely nearly 16.78 million colors. Human eyes are able to distinguish up to 10 million colors, but naming and remembering all is a task not for an average mind. For instance, most people employ just 11 color words: red, pink, orange, brown, yellow, green, blue, purple, grey, white and black. Artists and designers frequently utilize between 50 and 100 color names, including terms such as taupe or turquoise. Considering the immense difference between the number of colors we perceive and the number of words we use to describe them, the dictionary size of 1,800 appears like a fair compromise between the practicability and completeness.


Artista Color Dictionary

The presented color space is split into 8 color groups: red, orange, yellow, green, aqua (or cyan), blue, purple and magenta. In each group, there are 4 categories related to color brightness and saturation. Vivid colors are of a high saturation and a high brightness, and dark vivid colors are of a low brightness. Pastel colors are of a low saturation and a high brightness, and dark pale colors are of a low saturation and a low brightness.
In the Artista Color Dictionary, each color is provided with its respective shades (adding black), tints (adding white) and tones (varying saturation). These color variations are commonly used in design to create depth and extend color palettes. Artista also gives recommendations on color combinations using an in-house algorithm, which is based on an extensive literature research and designers' color recommendations data. Similar colors are listed to encourage a color exploration.


Colors Matter

Research has repeatedly shown that colors matter a great deal as they evoke associations and can trigger strong emotions. Most colors are actually named after objects, such as tomato, lemon, orange, coral, gold or zircon. A look at the lemon-colored swatch may even trigger a sour taste in some people.
An interesting piece of research uncovered that is much easier for us to communicate and describe warm colors (red, orange, yellow) than cool colors (green, blue). A plausible explanation is that cool colors usually constitute the background (blue sky, green grass, blue water) and warm colors naturally appear as objects in the foreground. Thus, an object in a warm color placed on a cool background will naturally stand out to us. This is one of many insights that can be employed in digital design to create appealing contents aligned with our color perception principles and associations.
